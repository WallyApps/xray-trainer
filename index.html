The 3-second scan

Every new person you talk to today: Feet → Hands → Blink. Three seconds, every single time, until it runs without you. Here's how to read what you see:

Feet pointed at you = engaged · at the exit = they want out · at one person = that's the leader
Hands loose, open fingers = comfortable, agreeing · curled or tense = stressed, disagreeing
Blink fast = stressed · barely blinking = locked in · sudden change = something just hit them

Habit-stacking on every greeting is what makes profiling automatic instead of something you remember to do.

Flow, defined

Flow is what happens when your body feels safe and your attention is fully outside your own head. Chase's whole system — self-forgiveness, no hierarchy, observation mode — is a recipe for exactly that. You don't chase flow. You remove what blocks it.

Game-day protocol
Pre-game

Before you step on the ice

1. Say your identity line: "I'm the type of player that…" — and finish it the same way every game.
2. Catch every "what if I mess up" loop and cancel it with one rep of the same moment going right. Worry is rehearsal for failure — out-rep it.
3. Step into the role. A firefighter runs into buildings because the role grants permission. Your role: pro. Play with its permission.
4. Pool speed in the room and in warmups. Slow hands, slow head turns. Composure = authority, and your own body is watching you.
